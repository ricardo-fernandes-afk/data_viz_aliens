When the Sky Went Crazy
Since the 1940s, thousands of UFO sightings have been reported across the United States and beyond. But the sky doesn’t go wild every year—some peaks stand out.

In 1997, the infamous Phoenix Lights event lit up Arizona’s sky (and the national news). In 2020, Pentagon videos revived public interest.

This timeline shows how UFO reports spiked in waves—perhaps reflecting cultural moments, not cosmic arrivals?
When the Sky Went Crazy
🔹 Phoenix Lights: Mass UFO sighting over Arizona sparks nationwide interest.
🔹 Post-9/11 Spike: Heightened surveillance and military activity may have boosted sightings.
🔹 USS Nimitz: Fighter pilots encounter unidentified objects during routine exercises.
ARE WE SEEING MORE UFOS—OR JUST WATCHING MORE NEWS?
If sightings rise with media attention… when exactly do people look up into the sky?

Aliens Love
Summer Nights

UFOs prefer the warm season. Coincidence? Maybe not.
Most reports occur in summer months—especially July and August. Longer nights, more outdoor time, or just more imagination in the air?

It’s not just the season—time of day matters too.
When the sun sets, the sightings start.

After Dark, Before Proof

Reports rise sharply after 8 PM and peak around midnight. Maybe that’s when aliens visit—or when we start seeing what we want to see.

SO WE KNOW WHEN
PEOPLE LOOK UP.
BUT WHERE DO
THEY LOOK FROM?

Hotspots on Earth

Not all skies are equal. The majority of sightings come from the United States—by far.

Within the US, states like California, Florida, and Texas top the list. Why? High population, clear weather, a culture of curiosity… or just better PR for aliens?

So we know where sightings are reported. But what do people actually see?

Triangles, Fireballs and Lights:
The Changing Face of UFOs

UFOs don’t just show up—they evolve.

In the 1960s, flying saucers ruled the sky. But in the 2000s, it’s mostly lights, fireballs and triangles.

Are aliens upgrading their vehicles—or are we just describing what we expect to see?

SHAPES CHANGE
BUT HOW LONG

Blink and
They‘re Gone?
UFO Sightings
by Duration

Most UFO encounters are over in a flash. Over 60% of sightings last less than 5 minutes, many even under a minute.

But duration depends on the type. “Triangles” and “spheres” tend to linger, while “lights” often vanish quickly.

Are these differences technical—or psychological?
We may never know, but the data points to something more than randomness.

Could timing also relate
to special dates?

Some dates seem to draw more than just crowds—they draw eyes to the sky. UFO sightings consistently spike on holidays, especially the loud and light-filled ones. Independence Day leads by far—coincidence, or something more?

Are celebrations masking what’s truly unusual, or are we simply more likely to look up when fireworks fly? Whatever the reason, the calendar seems to matter.
